# Main Prompt

IMPORTANT PRE-GENERATION RULE:
A reference image is required. If no reference image is attached, visible, or available, do not generate anything. Stop and ask the user to provide the reference image first. Do not invent a scene from this prompt alone.

Use the provided reference image as the primary source for the scene, environment, subject matter, visual style, mood, lighting, materials, era, weather, color palette, and overall atmosphere.

Create a fully immersive 360-degree equirectangular panoramic image in a 2:1 aspect ratio, suitable for VR panorama viewing.

The final image should feel as if the viewer has stepped inside the world shown in the reference image. Do not simply widen, stretch, or copy the original image. Instead, expand it into a complete surrounding environment that logically continues in every direction.

Viewer and camera:
Place the viewer at natural human eye height near the center of the scene. The viewer should be able to freely look forward, backward, left, right, upward, and downward. The panorama must include the complete surrounding space: front, back, left side, right side, ground or floor, and sky or ceiling.

Projection and panorama rules:
Use proper equirectangular projection. The image must be a true 2:1 panoramic environment, not a normal wide-angle photograph. Keep the horizon level, stable, and naturally positioned. The left and right edges must connect seamlessly with no visible seam, border, mismatch, lighting jump, repeated object, broken geometry, or perspective discontinuity.

Scene expansion:
Infer what exists beyond the frame of the reference image and build a believable full world around the viewer. Preserve the reference image's main visual language, including its tone, realism level, atmosphere, lighting direction, texture quality, and emotional mood. Add surrounding details that fit naturally with the original scene.

Spatial depth:
Create strong foreground, midground, and background layers. Include close-up ground or floor details near the viewer, larger environmental structures or natural features around the middle distance, and distant background elements with atmospheric depth. The space should feel continuous, walkable, physically believable, and fully surrounding.

Lighting and materials:
Use cinematic realistic lighting, high dynamic range HDR, natural shadow transitions, realistic material textures, atmospheric perspective, subtle volumetric light, believable reflections where appropriate, and physically plausible light behavior. The lighting should match the reference image while still working naturally across the full 360-degree environment.

Detail and realism:
Make the environment rich with small believable details, but do not overcrowd the scene. Add realistic surface texture, environmental wear, natural imperfections, small objects, depth cues, and background atmosphere. The final result should feel immersive, high-resolution, ultra-detailed, and photographic or visually consistent with the reference style.

Seam safety:
Avoid placing important characters, faces, readable text, major buildings, or highly detailed objects directly on the left-right seam. Use continuous background elements, ground texture, sky, walls, landscape, or atmospheric haze near the seam so the panorama wraps naturally.

Technical requirements:
2:1 aspect ratio, equirectangular projection, seamless 360-degree panorama, stable horizon, human eye-level viewpoint, complete surrounding environment, immersive VR panorama, strong spatial depth, high detail, high resolution, realistic or reference-matching style.

Avoid:
ordinary wide-angle photo, fisheye lens distortion, flat poster composition, single front-facing view, cropped environment, incomplete surroundings, visible stitching, mismatched left and right edges, duplicated seam objects, warped horizon, distorted people, unreadable text, random objects that do not match the reference, camera rigs, tripod shadows, watermark, captions, labels, UI elements, or anything that breaks the illusion of a real 360-degree environment.
